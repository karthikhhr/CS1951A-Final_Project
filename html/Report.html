Analyzing Economic Growth in Sub-Saharan Africa

In this report, we take a data-based approach in trying to determine how particular factors influenced growth in those parts of Africa south of the Sahara desert.

Intro and Hypothesis

The plethora of datasets available for macroeconomic analysis and the potential policy ramifications of such research make economics an important discipline with the potential to apply data science concepts. While statistical methods such as regression are commonplace in econometric analysis, we believe that there is massive scope for the use of more advanced statistical machine learning techniques to analyze vast amounts of known data and make predictions and recommendations for economists and policy makers. We chose to focus on the part of Africa south of the Sahara which has traditionally been one of the poorest regions in the world. However, it has vast amounts of untapped economic potential that are slowly being exploited, leading to increasing growth rates over the last few years. We believe that the use of proper statistical analysis tools can help identify factors that would continue to contribute to growth based on past trends.

By accounting for factors such as consumption, capital formation and development aid, we hypothesize that all the factors that are considered would contribute positively to GDP growth, with the exception of development aid. We expect development aid to have a negative impact, if there is any impact at all, while foreign direct investment would have a disproportionately high positive impact. We also think it is reasonable to believe that effects on GDP growth for the next year will be consistent with GDP growth data for the year after, i.e. a 2 year lag.

        

Relevant literature

Regression is a commonly used tool in econometric analysis since it helps quantify the extent to which certain variables depend on other variables. As a result, there are numerous academic works involving the use of regression with various factors to analyze GDP growth. Additionally, we also found one academic work that attempted to analyze economic growth with the use of Support Vector Machine techniques.

  • 2000 Determinants of Economic Growth in East Asia: A Linear Regression Model Elizabeth Kowalski '00 Illinois Wesleyan University
  • Aid and growth regressions Henrik Hansen , Finn Tarp DERG, University of Copenhagen, Denmark
  • Time Series Forecasting for Economic Growth based on Particle Swarm Optimization and Support Vector Machine Long Gang Economics and Management School of Wuhan University

Dataset

The data was obtained by the World Bank’s DataBank website, an excellent resource for economic and development data. While initially data was obtained for a variety of features for 54 countries in the time period 1960-2012, this was ultimately pruned to 1986-2012 for 20 countries for 9 attributes. This is because there were large amounts of missing data for various countries across attributes. Eventually we settled on a time range that was large enough to provide a good estimate, included certain important features and countries, yet was recent enough for us to be reasonably confident about the predicted trends.

Procedure and formulae

Cleaning the Dataset

The data was, conveniently, available to download as a CSV file from the World Bank DataBank website. However, there were large gaps in much of the data. To make matters worse, the gaps in the data were widely scattered across different countries, time frames and attributes which made it harder to refine the data with strict cutoff points.

We began with data for 48 different countries across 30 different attributes from a timeframe of 1960-2011. These included various measures quantified in different units, such as constant 2005 USD, percentage of GDP, and so on. The challenge was to find a tradeoff between retaining as many countries across as wide range a time period as possible without compromising on the diversity of attributes available to analyze. Conversely, a large number of attributes would restrict the number of countries and timeframes that we could use since there would be little overlap.

  1. We began by parsing the CSV file and creating a three-dimensional dictionary (a dictionary of dictionaries of dictionaries) so that at any point, we could access a country’s value for a particular metric for any given year while keeping track of the holes in the data as well by marking them with an “NA”. We also had separate lists to keep track of the countries and metrics that we were going to end up using.
  2. We then looked at how many holes each country had for each metric, one metric at a time. This helped us initially rule out some metrics - for instance, almost no countries had any kind of data available for literacy rate, gini index, debt-to-GDP ration, etc.
  3. Once we had pruned the list of potential metrics, we looked at which countries had sufficient data for the new list of metrics. We decided to eliminate all those countries that had missing data for more than 35 years straight off the bat. The number 35 is fairly arbitrary, but we settled on it after some experimentation and balancing the trade-off between important countries and metrics that we would miss out on versus ensuring that we got an adequately large sample for each metric and country that we did decide to include.
  4. Even after getting a list of countries that had data for a certain number of years, however, we had to eliminate some countries whose data was not continuous and hence not usable.
  5. There were still a couple of holes remaining in the data. We filled them in using interpolation of surrounding points
  6. Official development aid data was in the form of constant 2012 USD. We converted this to a percentage of GDP by dividing it by the total GDP in constant 2005 USD. Even though the USD had different valuations at these years, the ratio 2012USD/2005USD is a constant and hence moves linearly.

Ultimately, the countries analyzed were geographically distributed across the continent and exhibited widely varying levels of GDP growth in recent years, thus ensuring that a diverse selection.

Simple Regression (Dimensionality of Input Space=1) to analyze growth.

We used annual GDP Growth (Annual %) as a direct indicator of a country’s growth in a particular year. In this part of analysis, we ran linear regressions on a total of six independent variables individually mapping each one of them to the only dependent variable (GDP Growth(Annual %)). The independent variables were:

  • Final Consumption Expenditure (% of GDP)
  • Exports of Goods and Services (% of GDP)
  • Population Growth (Annual %)
  • Population, Total
  • Net Official Development Assistance and Official Aid Received (% of GDP)
  • Foreign Direct Investment, Net Inflows (% of GDP)

However we did this process for every country twice; once by considering the dependent variables with a one-year lag, and once with a two-year lag. This was done to account for the fact that economic factors in a particular year do not influence always add up consummately to produce an effect in that year or the immediate next year’s growth. It could be very well true that an economic factor of the current year might only affect the growth from two years later. However, we would like to mention that our original hypothesis did not expect a vast difference in the growth curves in the two methods based on the empirical fact that the independent variables themselves do not change much in a period as small as two years.

Multiple Linear Regression (Dimensionality of Input Space=2) to analyze growth.

As in the previous part, we continued to used annual GDP Growth (Annual %) as a direct metric of a country’s growth. However, in this part, we ran regressions on n-dimensional input spaces (n being six and five respectively) and mapped the feature vectors to their corresponding dependent variable i.e. GDP Growth (Annual %). We did the process in two steps with two different input feature spaces. One feature space included Population, Total and the other did not.

Therefore the two input feature vectors for every country for each year looked like this :

<Final Consumption Expenditure (% of GDP), Exports of Goods and Services (% of GDP), Population Growth (Annual %), Population, Total, Net Official Development Assistance and Official Aid Received (% of GDP), Foreign Direct Investment, Net Inflows (% of GDP)>

and

<Final Consumption Expenditure (% of GDP), Exports of Goods and Services (% of GDP), Population Growth (Annual %), Net Official Development Assistance and Official Aid Received (% of GDP), Foreign Direct Investment, Net Inflows (% of GDP)>

This separation was done in order to ensure that we look at all percentages in one method for better visualization. ‘Population, Total’, being a large value would almost always make the mesh grid skewed and in a way biased to incorporate the larger value and thus accurate depiction of the growth phenomenon was not entirely possible with such a feature space. Our original hypothesis did expect a difference in the growth curves in the two methods.

Internally, for each of these two methods, we made a further distinction in how the regression itself was performed. In one case, we performed the regression on the five/six dimensional feature space directly while in the other we reduced the dimensionality of the feature space using Principal Component Analysis and regressed over the 2-D feature space thus created by mapping it against the lone dependent variable. This further distinction was made to test the vulnerability of a small feature space versus a large one in the face of limited data. We originally hypothesised that a larger feature space would generate the appropriate amount of variance we need for the model to be able to predict future trends successfully when used for such purposes.


Two-Class Support Vector Machine (Input Space Dimensionality =1) to Predict the Better Contributor to Various Economic Factors: Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) or Official Development Assistance (ODA).

The main idea behind this classifier was to try to judge what economic factors are influenced considerably by these two forms of fiscal inflows into each country and to compare one’s contribution to the country’s growth over the other. For each country data was collected for all the years being considered (1986-2011) and the SVM was trained on each input variable separately while classifying it as either 0 or 1, where 0 means “due to ODA” and 1 means “due to FDI”. The input data looked like this:

  • GDP Growth (Annual %)
  • Final Consumption Expenditure (% of GDP)
  • Exports of Goods and Services (% of GDP)
  • Population Growth (Annual %)
  • Gross National Expenditure (% of GDP)

Our original hypothesis was that this method would not be successful because of the extremely high bias in the model. This would be because of the fact that for almost all countries the percentage of GDP in the form of FDI is always greater than that in the form of ODA. Though this might be the case numerically, this model offers no insight into how much these inputs truly contribute to the growth. This model would be too simple to use in this complicated scenario. We therefore turned to other more complex Support Vector Machines to answer similar questions albeit in a roundabout manner.

Multi-Class Support Vector Machine (Input Space Dimensionality = 5) to Predict which of one of the following Economic Factors is Expected to Grow: Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) or Official Development Assistance (ODA).

The idea behind this classifier was derived from the idea of the previous simple SVM where we wanted to find a correlation between a country’s economic scenario and the fiscal inflows into the country in the form of FDI and ODA. We hypothesize that this model would be much more accurate in helping us gain an insight into these dependencies and even suggest a way forward for the continent in terms of economic strategy ( at least on an average) for the immediate next few years. One of the biggest challenges in attempting to build a SVM over the input feature space is its high dimensionality. This would make it really difficult to visualize and present the results. However, we know that since the current dimensionality is five, there would be five pairs of eigenvectors and eigenvalues (variances of the data in the direction of the corresponding eigenvectors). As before, we employed Principal Component Analysis to scale down the dimensionality from five to two. Essentially, in doing so, we found the two eigenvectors such that their eigenvalues or variances are the greatest while ignoring those three eigenvectors with small/negligible variances that do not describe the data in much detail. The following is the plot of the eigenvalues from our data.

The five input variables gave way to two additional ‘input variables’ which are actually the two Principal Components which account for all the existing variables in the 5-D Space. This was of course done for every country for all years except the last one (1986-2010 i.e. except 2011). This projection of the 5-D space to 2-D helped us easily train our Multi-Class SVM over the input data with relatively less bias. Lesser bias means that the model would be a good predictor of future trends. The input space used in the model was

<GDP Growth (Annual %), Final Consumption Expenditure (% of GDP), Exports of Goods and Services (% of GDP), Population Growth (Annual %), Gross National Expenditure (% of GDP)>

which became

<Principal Component 1, Principal Component 2>

after applying PCA.

There were four different models of such Multi-Class Support Vector Machines that were built for the training and prediction. They are:

  • Multi-Class Linear Support Vector Machines (that follow One vs All classification)
  • Multi-Class Linear Support Vector Machines (that follow One vs One classification)
  • Multi-Class Radial Basis Function (Kernel) Support Vector Machines
  • Multi-Class Third Degree Polynomial Support Vector Machines


It was hypothesized that not all models would be successful in predicting the future trends. This would mean that there would not be a single best model for the job and that some models might work better depending on the situation. However, it is worth noting that our hypothesis did attribute a greater chance of success for the kernel SVM and for the polynomial SVM. Between the two Linear SVMs our hypothesis does not expect either to work better than the other. Both the models face their set of complications and issues. In particular, for the One vs All case, it is common to encounter several examples where we find regions of input space that are ambiguously classified. The way this approach works is that the SVM tries to classify the input space as if it were a two class problem for each class, i.e. it simply does ‘Class’ or ‘Not Class’. On the other hand the way the One vs One case works is that the SVM tries to classify each input point according to the maximum score calculated amongst the discriminant functions. This classifier too is not bereft of ambiguous regions. While this surely means that the data is biased, basing on this (failed) model, we can therefore conclude that FDI in fact is a much bigger contributor to the growth in Sub-Saharan Africa compared to Foreign Aid. Foreign aid on the other hand may not always be utilized to drive markets or other economic processes and hence may not be such a big factor in the country’s economic development.

Multi-Class SVM

Insofar as we made it clear that the failure of the previous SVM model suggests that FDI always almost contributes in a bigger way to countries’ overall growth patterns, we move on to understand how growth or the lack thereof in FDI or ODA is brought about in the current year by analyzing a country’s economic factors in the previous year. Not only do we understand but we also aim to make predictions for some countries for the year 2013 and verify the results with the actual changes. For this purpose we use a multi-class svm with the following classes as previously mentioned:

  • Class 0 : “Neither FDI nor ODA is expected to grow” : YELLOW
  • Class 1 : “Only FDI is expected to grow” : GREEN
  • Class 2 : “Only ODA is expected to grow” : RED
  • Class 3 : “Both FDI and ODA are expected to grow" : BLUE

From the above visualizations, it is clear that the models tried its best to classify the limited data points to the classes they belong to. But it has to be noted that the model still exhibits considerable bias in that not all points are classified accurately on the training data itself. This could have been reduced by running K-Fold cross validation but the number of data points are too limited for that process. The coordinates (PC1,PC2) as described previously represent a 5-D data point which is the vector of economic factors of a country during a particular year. The classes are assigned to these points by the SVM by referring to the labels that we had manually associated with each vector by looking at the original growth trends. As hypothesized earlier, no model works the best for any one country. From the fifteen examples provided here, it can be seen that while RBG Kernel saw some success in classifying the data points of Burkina Faso, Polynomial SVM performed relatively better on Cameroon. Similarly Linear One vs All performed slightly better on Botswana compared to the other countries while Linear One vs One did the same on Mauritius compared to the rest.

It should be noted (as done before) that Equatorial Guinea and Rwanda almost consistently poorly across all the different classifiers. This behavior can also be attributed to the outliers in the original data set in the year 1997 for Equatorial Guinea and in the year 1994 for Rwanda.

These discrepancies are most likely due to the variance in the feature coordinates. Since all the models are inherently biased, they react sharply to even small changes in the magnitude of the input coordinates and therefore make mistakes in the initial classification on training data. It is therefore safe to assume that similar discrepancies will be observed even for predictive purposes for the countries for the year 2013. The nature of the discrepancies cannot be determined because of the sheer randomness in the feature coordinate variances and the inherently bias nature of each of the classifiers.

While our regression models did a good job of predicting the changes in the GDP for all the countries and mapping the existing trends, our SVM models sought to make use of these established trends as input vectors in order to output (classify) them as the independent variables/ coordinates of the independent feature variable spaces of the regression models. Moving forward

The complex interplay between all the different variables makes analysis difficult. It is hard to determine which variables can be left out and which ones need to be controlled for. If more data is available, the above techniques should be run on as many combinations of variables as possible, used to predict future trends and then matched against the actual values. Additionally, findings should be compared against those from other countries to analyze how different factors have different levels of influence in different regions of the world. Lastly, countries should perhaps be clustered together using various economic and geographical metrics so as to compare similar economies when predicting trends rather than a relatively random smattering of countries.
